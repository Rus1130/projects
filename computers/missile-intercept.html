1. The Guidance Law: Augmented Proportional Navigation (APN)

The interceptor utilizes Augmented Proportional Navigation to calculate its flight path. Unlike direct pursuit, which chases the target's current position, APN commands acceleration based on the rotation of the Line-of-Sight (LOS) between the missile and the target.

nc = N · Vc · λ′ + (0.5 · N · gtarget)

Where N is the navigation constant (set to 4.5–6.5), Vc is the closing velocity, and λ′ is the Line-of-Sight rate. The "Augmented" term accounts for the ballistic missile's downward acceleration due to gravity, allowing the SAM to "lead" the target into its future position more accurately.

2. Ballistic Trajectory Dynamics

The incoming threat mimics a Short-Range Ballistic Missile (SRBM) in its terminal re-entry phase. The physics engine accounts for:

  • Gravitational Acceleration: Constant -9.81 m/s2 pull, causing a parabolic arc toward the designated ground target.
  • Atmospheric Drag: Drag is calculated using the square of the velocity (Fd = 0.5 ρ v2 Cd A), causing the missile to lose velocity as it enters denser air at lower altitudes.

3. Kinematic Constraints and G-Loading

To maintain physical realism, the SAM is governed by structural G-limits. A real-world airframe would disintegrate under infinite lateral force. In this simulation, the SAM is capped at 42Gs of lateral acceleration.

This constraint creates a Minimum Turning Radius. If the SAM is traveling at high Mach numbers, it cannot make sharp course corrections, necessitating a high-energy "Vertical Launch" to gain altitude where the air is thinner before initiating the terminal intercept maneuver.

4. Intercept Logic (Proximity Fuzing)

Because closing speeds often exceed Mach 6 (combined), a "skin-to-skin" collision is mathematically difficult to calculate at standard frame rates. The simulation utilizes a Proximity Fuze logic. When the distance falls below 50 meters, the missile detonates its blast-fragmentation warhead, resulting in a confirmed intercept.
